Types of Donation

The human body holds approximately five liters of blood, a vital elixir composed of Red Blood Cells, Plasma, and Platelets. Each component plays a distinct role, offering the power to save countless lives.

What is it?

Blood drawn from a donor is blended with an anticoagulant, then spun in a centrifuge to isolate red cells, platelets, and plasma. Preserved red cells become packed red blood cells.

Who can donate?

Healthy individuals aged 18-65, weighing 45kg or more.

Used For?

Treats severe anemia, blood loss from childbirth, surgery, or trauma.

How long does it take?

15-30 minutes, including pre-donation screening.

Storage Duration

42 days at 2-6°C.

What is it?

Platelets, the guardians of clotting, are essential for those with low counts.

Who can donate?

Like whole blood donors, with a platelet count above 150,000 per microliter.

Used For?

Supports cancer patients, blood disorder cases, and trauma recovery.

How long does it take?

45-60 minutes for donation, 2-3 hours including screening.

Storage Duration

5 days at 20-24°C.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can donate blood?

Healthy individuals aged 18-65, weighing at least 45kg, with no recent illnesses, tattoos, or piercings, and no high-risk behaviors, can donate. A hemoglobin test is required to ensure eligibility.

How often can I donate blood?

Whole blood can be donated every 56 days (8 weeks). Platelet or plasma donations may allow more frequent donations (every 7-14 days) depending on your health and donation type, as advised by a doctor.

Does donating blood hurt?

The process involves a small needle prick, which may cause mild discomfort. The donation itself is painless, and any soreness usually subsides within a day. Rest and hydration help recovery.

What should I do after donating blood?

Rest for 10-15 minutes, drink plenty of water, eat a snack, and avoid heavy lifting for the rest of the day. Contact a healthcare provider if you feel dizzy or unwell.

Can I donate if I have a chronic condition?

It depends on the condition. Conditions like controlled hypertension or diabetes may be acceptable, but diseases like cancer, heart disease, or hepatitis disqualify you. Consult your doctor and the donation center.
